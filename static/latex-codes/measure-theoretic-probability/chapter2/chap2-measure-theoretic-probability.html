2 Independence

Let \((\Omega , \mathcal{F}, P)\) be a probability space.

2.1 Pairwise independece

DeÔ¨Ånition 2.1. Two events (elements of \(\mathcal{F}\)) \(A\) and \(B\) are independent if \(P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B)\).

DeÔ¨Ånition 2.2. Two random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent if for all \(C, D \in \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}}\),

i.e. the events \(A = \{X \in C \}\) and \(B = \{Y \in D\}\) are independent.

DeÔ¨Ånition 2.3. Two (sub)\(\sigma \)-algebras (of \(\mathcal{F}\)) \(\mathcal{F}_1\) and \(\mathcal{F}_2\) are independent if for all \(A \in \mathcal{F}_1\) and \(B \in \mathcal{F}_2\), the events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent.

The second deÔ¨Ånition is a special case of the third (in the sense that \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent iÔ¨Ä \(\sigma (X)\) and \(\sigma (Y)\) are independent):

Theorem 2.4.

1.
If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent r.v.‚Äôs then so are \(\sigma (X)\) and \(\sigma (Y)\).
2.
Conversely, if \(X\), \(Y\) are r.v.‚Äôs, \(\mathcal{F}_1\) and \(\mathcal{F}_2\) are independent, \(X\) is \(\mathcal{F}_1\)-measurable and \(Y\) is \(\mathcal{F}_2\)-measurable, then \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent.

Proof

1.
Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be independent r.v.‚Äôs. \(\sigma (X)\) is the smallest \(\sigma \)-algebra that makes \(X\) measurable and its elements are the sets \(X^{-1}(B)\) for \(B \in \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}}\). Similarly, \(\sigma (Y)\) is the sets of sets of the form \(Y^{-1}(B)\) for \(B \in \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}}\).

Hence, it is suÔ¨Écient to prove that the sets \(X^{-1}(B)\) and \(Y^{-1}(C)\) are independent for all \(B,C \in \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}}\). But this is immediate from the deÔ¨Ånition of independent random variables.

2.
Let \(C, D \in \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}}\). Then, \(X^{-1}(C) \in \mathcal{F}_1\) and \(Y^{-1}(D) \in \mathcal{F}_2\). Since these two sigma algebras are independent, it follows that \(X^{-1}(C)\) and \(Y^{-1}(D)\) are independent.

\(\blacksquare \)

The Ô¨Årst deÔ¨Ånition is, in turn, a special case of the second (in the sense that \(A\) and \(B\) are independent iÔ¨Ä \(1_A\) and \(1_B\) are independent):

Theorem 2.5.

1.
If \(A\) and \(B\) are independent, then so are \(A^c\) and \(B\), \(A\) and \(B^c\), and \(A^c\) and \(B^c\).
2.
Events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent iÔ¨Ä \(1_A\) and \(1_B\) are independent.

2.2 n-wise independence

More generally, we can deÔ¨Åne independence for more than two events, r.v.‚Äôs, or \(\sigma \)-algebras.

DeÔ¨Ånition 2.6. \(\sigma \)-algebras \(\mathcal{F}_1, \mathcal{F}_2, \ldots , \mathcal{F}_n\) are independent if whenever \(A_i \in \mathcal{F}_i\) for \(i = 1, \ldots , n\), we have

\[ P\left (\bigcap _{i=1}^n A_i\right ) = \prod _{i=1}^n P(A_i) \]

DeÔ¨Ånition 2.7.

Random variables \(X_1, \ldots , X_n\) are independent if whenever \(B_i \in \mathcal{B}_\mathbb{R}\) for \(i = 1, \ldots , n\), we have \[ P\left (\bigcap _{i=1}^n X_i^{-1}(B_i)\right ) = \prod _{i=1}^n P(X_i \in B_i) \]

DeÔ¨Ånition 2.8.

Sets \(A_1, \ldots , A_n\) are independent if whenever \(I \subset \{1, \ldots , n\}\), we have \[ P\left (\bigcap _{i \in I} A_i\right ) = \prod _{i \in I} P(A_i) \]

You may think this last deÔ¨Ånition looks ‚Äùtoo diÔ¨Äerent‚Äù from the others. However, it is perfectly Ô¨Åne:

Lemma 2.9. If \(A_1, \dots , A_n\) are independent then so are \(A_1^c, \dots , A_n\).

Theorem 2.10. Events \(A_1, \dots , A_n\) are independent iÔ¨Ä \(1_{A_1}, \dots , 1_{A_n}\) are independent.

Theorem 2.11. Random variables \(X_1, \dots , X_n\) are independent iÔ¨Ä \(\sigma (X_1), \dots , \sigma (X_n)\) are independent.

Remark 2.12. Pairwise independence is NOT the same as independence.

2.3 Countable independece

DeÔ¨Ånition 2.13. Events \(A_1, A_2, \dots \) are independent if for all Ô¨Ånite positive integer sequence \(s_1, \dots , s_n\) the events \(A_{s_1}, \dots , A_{s_n}\) are independent. We deÔ¨Åne independece for random variables and sigma algebras analogously.

Theorem 2.14. Events \(A_1, A_2, \dots \) are independent iÔ¨Ä r.v.‚Äôs \(1_{A_1}, 1_{A_2}, \dots \) are independent.

Theorem 2.15. Random variables \(X_1, X_2, \dots \) are independent iÔ¨Ä \(\sigma (X_1), \sigma (X_2), \dots \) are independent.

Proof
Let \(\mathcal{F}_{i, j} = \sigma (X_{i, j})\) and \(\mathcal{G}_i = \sigma (\cup _j \mathcal{F}_{i, j})\).

Let \(V_i\) denote the random vector \((X_{i, 1}, \dots , X_{i, m(i)})\) and \(S_i =\) sets of the form \(\{ \omega : V_i \in A_1 \times \dots \times A_{m(i)} \}\) where each \(A_j\) is Borel. This means that \(\sigma (S_i)\) is precisely the smallest \(\sigma \)-algebra that makes \(V_i\) measurable i.e. \(\sigma (S_i) = \sigma (V_i)\).

Also, notice that \(\{ \omega : V_i \in A_1 \times \dots \times A_{m(i)} \} =\) \(\{\omega : \forall 1 \leq j \leq m(i), X_{i,j} \in A_j \} = \) \(\cap _j X_{i, j}^{-1}(A_j) \). This means that \(G_i = f_i(X_{i, 1}, \dots , X_{i, m(i)})\) is \(\mathcal{G}_i\) measurable, because if \(B_1 \in \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}}\), then \(f_i^{-1}(B_1) = B_2 \in \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}^{m(i)}}\). Since each \(G_i\) is \(\mathcal{G}_i\) measurable, we have \(\sigma (G_i) \subset \mathcal{G}_i\). So by the last theorem, the \(\mathcal{G}_i\) are independent. Therefore, the \(\sigma (G_i)\) are also independent and hence the \(G_i\) are independent. \(\blacksquare \)
